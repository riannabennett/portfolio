Crafting Identity


While completing my Master of Architecture, my thesis explored the history of industry in New Orleans while arguing for the necessity of ornament in contemporary urban design.
View Project

Intro

The rich history of industry and craft in New Orleans has become clouded by the recent economic reliance on tourism. Careful analysis of the potential semiotics of architecture provides the opportunity to reshape the meaning, form, or expression of ornament without compromising the regional language and vernacular of the historical context. This project proposes that architecture should highlight and enhance this mix of desires, allowing each to benefit from it’s uniqueness. The Hubig’s Pies Factory in the historic Marigny became a cultural anchor in the residential area since 1922. After a devastating fire in July 2012, this project considers the potential for a new factory, uniting disparate tourist and industrial programs.

Material Constraints

A standard 6” x 6” ceramic tile module, used in the historic factory, became a feature through out the building. These forms were adapted and extruded, creating six tile types used to create variation in visibility, access, and light quality, interfacing with the public and factory components.

Ornament + Function

The tile adaptation leads the visitor through the factory, allowing for glimpses into the iconic pie making process. On the factory level, this tile provides utilitarian qualities required in this building typology: waterproof, antibacterial, resilient, and affordable.

Varied Application

The use of a flexible modular system allowed for strategic control of the wall system in different design moments. The deepest, angled tiles would direct and control the visitors view into the musem while the shallow tiles would allow for the most visibility.
